Critical Reasoning

An argument is a machine. The conclusion is the output. The premises are the inputs. The assumption is the unstated wire that connects them — and the GMAT's whole game is making you see it.

Critical Reasoning is structural, not verbal

A common mistake is to treat CR as a "reading" section. It is not. The reading is trivial — every CR passage is short and written in plain English. What CR tests is your ability to x-ray a paragraph and see the logical machinery underneath. The same machinery shows up in business pitches, scientific claims, op-eds, and your boss's powerpoint. Once you can see it, you cannot unsee it.

This chapter is the longest chapter in the book because it is the one that will pay you forever — the analytical reflexes built here transfer directly into every white-collar job. The GMAT just happens to be the test that grades them.

The CR core in five lines

  • Argument = Premise(s) + Assumption + Conclusion.
  • The conclusion is what the author wants you to believe. Find it first, before anything else.
  • The assumption is what the author needs to be true for the premises to imply the conclusion.
  • "Strengthen" = make the assumption more likely. "Weaken" = make it less likely.
  • Pre-think the gap before reading the choices. The right answer is the one that matches your pre-thought gap, not the one that "sounds smart".

Argument anatomy — find the conclusion first

Every CR argument has three parts. Two are visible on the page; one is hidden.

ELI5 · two simple examples

Example 1 · the bridge. Imagine a bridge with two pillars. The first pillar is a fact ("the river is wide"). The second pillar is the conclusion ("therefore the bridge is safe to cross"). Between them is a hidden support structure — the assumption — that connects pillar 1 to pillar 2. If the assumption fails, the bridge collapses. CR is just "find the hidden support and check whether it holds."

Example 2 · explaining a friend's bad mood. Your friend is grumpy. You say, "she must be tired." That is your conclusion. The premise is "she is grumpy." The hidden assumption — the unspoken wire — is "tiredness is the most likely cause of grumpiness, not, say, hunger or a bad day at work." A weakener would be: "she just had 9 hours of sleep." Now the assumption fails, and the conclusion is in trouble.

Expert · the diagnostic order
  1. Read the question stem first. Knowing whether you are looking for an assumption vs. a flaw vs. a strengthener changes how you read the passage.
  2. Find the conclusion. Mark it. Words like "therefore", "thus", "so", "the company should", "we can conclude" all tag conclusions.
  3. List the premises. Everything else that is fact-asserted by the author.
  4. Articulate the gap. The gap is the unstated jump from the premises to the conclusion. That gap is the assumption.
  5. Then look at the choices. Eliminate aggressively.
Trap · the "first sentence" assumption

Students often assume the first sentence is the conclusion. It is usually a premise. The conclusion frequently sits at the end of the paragraph, sometimes hidden after a word like "however" or "thus". Find it explicitly before evaluating choices.

The CR question-type map

The seven types you will see

  • Assumption "the argument depends on which of the following?" → find the unstated wire
  • Strengthen "which most strengthens?" → reinforce the wire
  • Weaken "which most weakens?" → cut the wire
  • Flaw "the reasoning is flawed because…" → name the broken wire
  • Evaluate "to evaluate the argument, it would be most useful to know…" → identify the question whose answer determines the wire's existence
  • Paradox / Resolve "which of the following best explains the apparent contradiction?" → find the missing puzzle piece
  • Inference "which must be true based on the passage?" → tightest deduction; not adding new info
ELI5 · two simple examples

Example 1 · the magician's trick. Watching a magic trick is like reading a CR argument. Strengthen says "make the trick seem more real." Weaken says "expose the wire holding up the dove." Flaw says "name what was wrong about the trick." Different lenses on the same act.

Example 2 · debating a friend. Your friend says "we should buy the red car because it's faster." If you ask "but is faster better for our needs?" — that is finding the assumption. If you say "I bet it's also more expensive" — that is a weakener. If you say "you assumed faster automatically equals better, but we live in a city" — that is naming the flaw.

The pre-thinking drill

Pre-thinking is the single most important habit in CR. Before you read a single answer choice, you must produce your own answer to the question. The right choice will then match your pre-thought answer, often almost verbatim. The other choices are designed to look attractive only if you have not pre-thought.

Expert · the 30-second pre-thought

For an Assumption / Strengthen / Weaken question, ask yourself two questions before scanning answers:

  1. What is the gap? What unstated thing is the author taking for granted?
  2. What kind of fact would close (or break) that gap? Be specific — name the entity, the variable, the relationship.

Example. Argument: "Sales of luxury cars in Country X dropped 20% last year. Therefore, demand for luxury cars in Country X has fallen." The gap: maybe sales dropped because of supply (factory shutdown, tariffs) rather than demand. Pre-thought weakener: "supply was constrained last year." Now scan. The right answer says exactly this in different words.

ELI5 · two simple examples

Example 1 · grocery shopping with a list. You walk into a store with a written list ("milk, bread, eggs"). You ignore everything else. Pre-thinking is making that list before you walk into the answer aisle. Without a list, every shiny package looks like dinner.

Example 2 · ordering takeout with a friend. Before opening the menu, you both decide "we want noodles, not too spicy". Now you skim the menu fast and reject everything else. Without that pre-decision, you read the whole menu and waste 10 minutes. The CR answer choices are the menu. Decide before opening it.

The recurring flaw patterns

The GMAT recycles a small set of logical errors. Recognize the pattern and the flaw question is over in 30 seconds.

The seven recurring flaws

  • Correlation = Causation two things happen together; the author claims one caused the other
  • Alternate Cause the author ignores another plausible cause
  • Reverse Causation A caused B, but the author claims B caused A
  • Sample Bias the surveyed group is unrepresentative of the population the conclusion targets
  • Equivocation / Term Shift a word or category quietly changes meaning between premise and conclusion
  • Percent vs. Number percentage rises while raw count falls, or vice versa
  • Necessary vs. Sufficient author treats one as the other
Trap · "rate vs. raw"

"Country X's accident rate per 100,000 cars rose 5%; therefore, more accidents are happening in Country X." This may be false — if the number of cars dropped, the absolute count of accidents might be lower despite the higher rate. Whenever a premise gives a percent or rate and the conclusion gives a count (or vice versa), an alternate-cause weakener is almost always sitting in the answer choices.

Worked examples

Worked Example · 01 · Assumption

Argument: City Z's public bus ridership fell 15% last year. The mayor concludes that residents prefer driving cars to taking public buses. The argument depends on which of the following assumptions?

  1. Identify conclusion: residents prefer cars over buses.
  2. Identify premise: bus ridership fell 15%.
  3. Articulate the gap: ridership fall does not automatically mean preference shift. Maybe routes were cut, fares rose, or remote work boomed.
  4. Pre-thought assumption: the ridership drop was caused by a change in preferences, not by a supply or external factor.

Answer (typical wording): "The decline in bus ridership was not primarily caused by changes in bus availability, fare, or alternative travel patterns unrelated to preference."

Worked Example · 02 · Weaken

Argument: A new study found that office workers who took 5-minute walks every hour reported less back pain than those who did not. Therefore, taking short walks reduces back pain. Which of the following, if true, most weakens the argument?

  1. Conclusion: walking causes less back pain.
  2. Premise: workers who walked reported less pain.
  3. Gap: correlation ≠ causation. Maybe walkers were already healthier, or the study was self-selected.
  4. Pre-thought weakener: an alternate cause for the correlation.

Answer (typical wording): "Workers who already had no back pain were more likely to volunteer for the walking group, while those with chronic pain stayed seated."

Worked Example · 03 · Paradox

Argument: A bookstore raised the price of its most popular novel by 20%. In the following month, total revenue from that novel increased by 15%, even though the number of copies sold decreased. Which of the following best explains the increase in revenue?

  1. Recognize: paradox question. Two facts that appear to contradict.
  2. Pre-think: revenue = price × quantity. If price went up 20% and quantity went down by less than 17% (since 1.20 × 0.83 ≈ 1.00), revenue still rises.
  3. Look for an answer that quantifies how much sales fell.

Answer (typical wording): "Sales volume dropped by less than 5%, which was more than offset by the 20% price increase."

Explanations · with structural reasoning

Explanation · 01↑ back to Q01
Answer · C

The conclusion is that kiosks will produce a similar wait-time effect at the remaining locations. The hidden assumption is that those locations are comparable to the ones already studied. C states this directly. A, B, D, and E may be true but do not address the gap between "kiosks worked there" and "kiosks will work here too".

Explanation · 02↑ back to Q02
Answer · B

Classic alternate-cause weakener. The correlation between breakfast and lower weight could be explained by a third factor — irregular sleep schedules — that independently affects weight. This breaks the causal claim. A is wrong direction; C is irrelevant; D is too vague; E suggests another correlation, not a competing cause.

Explanation · 03↑ back to Q03
Answer · B

The argument equates "online searches" with "purchase intent". B breaks that equivalence by showing the searches were driven by criticism, not purchase intent. C is a red-herring competitor mention; A is irrelevant to effectiveness; D arguably strengthens; E is irrelevant.

Explanation · 04↑ back to Q04
Answer · B

Strengthen by ruling out alternate causes. Showing a similar city without the bus switch did not see the same improvement isolates the bus rollout as the differentiating factor. This is the structural pattern the GMAT loves: the "control city" / "comparison group" answer.

Explanation · 05↑ back to Q05
Answer · B

This is the self-selection / sample-bias flaw. The students who chose music lessons may differ from the rest in income, parental support, or motivation — any of which could independently raise math scores. B names this flaw exactly. The other choices either nitpick the test or attack premises rather than the reasoning gap.

Explanation · 06↑ back to Q06
Answer · B

Evaluate questions ask: "what fact would determine whether the conclusion holds?" The leap from mouse data to humans depends on whether the relevant biology is shared. B targets exactly that. A, C, D, and E are off-target — they do not determine whether the mouse result transfers to humans.

Explanation · 07↑ back to Q07
Answer · B

Per-customer revenue is not the same as profit. B inserts a counter-fact: urban stores' lower costs could make them more profitable despite lower per-customer revenue. The conclusion (that urban stores are less profitable) collapses. A discusses frequency, irrelevant to the per-customer figure; C, D, E are irrelevant.

Explanation · 08↑ back to Q08
Answer · B

The reasoning treats correlation between app usage and high spending as causal. B names the self-selection flaw: app users may already be the highest-spending segment, so converting low-spenders to the app may not change their spending. This is a textbook reverse-causation / selection effect.

Explanation · 09↑ back to Q09
Answer · B

Paradox: fewer flights, more passengers. Resolution: bigger aircraft (so capacity per flight is higher) plus a marketing campaign that raised demand. B accounts for both directions of the paradox. A explains a price effect but does not explain how more passengers fit; C and D do not explain the rise; E offers a comparison-period explanation but not for the rise.

Explanation · 10↑ back to Q10
Answer · B

A randomized controlled experiment is the gold-standard strengthener for any causal claim — it rules out self-selection and most confounders. B describes exactly that. D explains a possible mechanism but does not provide causal evidence. The others are irrelevant.
