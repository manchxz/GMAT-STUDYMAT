What Data Sufficiency actually tests

Data Sufficiency is the GMAT's signature question type. It does not ask you to find a numeric answer. It asks whether the information given is enough to find a unique numeric answer. The skill being tested is logical sufficiency, not arithmetic — and that flips the way you should solve.

Solving DS the wrong way — by computing each statement to a final number — wastes most of your time on questions where you only needed to check whether a unique answer exists. Solving DS the right way is faster, more reliable, and resistant to nearly every trap. This chapter teaches the right way.

The DS first principles

  • Do not solve. Determine sufficiency only.
  • Test each statement in isolation first. Treat them like separate experiments.
  • Combine only if both fail individually.
  • For Value questions: sufficient = exactly one numeric answer.
  • For Yes/No questions: sufficient = always yes or always no, never sometimes.

The AD / BCE grid

Every DS question has the same five answer choices, in the same order. Memorize them so you can run the procedure without looking at the choices.

The five answer choices

  • A Statement 1 alone is sufficient; Statement 2 alone is not.
  • B Statement 2 alone is sufficient; Statement 1 alone is not.
  • C Both together are sufficient; neither alone is.
  • D Each statement alone is sufficient.
  • E Together they are still not sufficient.
ELI5 · two simple examples

Example 1 · two clue cards. Imagine a detective gives you two clue cards about a hidden number. You read each card alone. If card 1 alone tells you the number, that's "sufficient by itself". If card 1 alone is too vague, but card 2 narrows it down, that's also sufficient. If neither card alone works but together they do, you need both. The five answers (A, B, C, D, E) are just five different stories about how the two cards interact with your ability to find the number.

Example 2 · solving a treasure map. The map has two halves. With just the top half, can you find the treasure? With just the bottom half, can you? With both? In real life, sometimes one half is enough, sometimes you need both, sometimes neither even together is enough. DS is exactly this question — except instead of treasure, you are looking for "exactly one possible value of x".

Expert · the elimination shortcut

The "AD / BCE" grouping reflects the fastest elimination strategy:

  1. Test Statement 1 alone. If sufficient, the answer is A or D. If not, B, C, or E.
  2. Then test Statement 2 alone. Combined with step 1, you can narrow to a single letter or two.
  3. Combine only if both individually fail. Then it is C vs. E.

This procedure prevents the most common mistake: prematurely combining the two statements before testing each alone.

Value questions vs. Yes/No questions

The two DS question types

  • Value "What is the value of x?" Sufficient = single numeric answer. Two possible values = NOT sufficient.
  • Yes/No "Is x > 5?" Sufficient = consistent answer (always yes, or always no). Mixed = NOT sufficient.
ELI5 · two simple examples

Example 1 · the locker number. Your friend asks you to find a locker number. If they give you a clue and you can name exactly one locker, that is enough. If two lockers fit the clue, it is not — you cannot pick one over the other. That is a value question.

Example 2 · is it raining? If your friend looks outside and says "yes" or "no" without flipping back and forth, that is a sufficient answer. If they say "well, it's raining on the left side of the house but sunny on the right", they have given you mixed information. That is a Yes/No question with insufficient data.

Trap · "yes/no" sometimes has a "yes" answer that means insufficient

For Yes/No questions, an answer of "sometimes yes, sometimes no" is insufficient — even if "yes" is one of the possible answers. Sufficiency means consistency, not just compatibility.

The DS procedure — every question, every time

Expert · the seven-step routine
  1. Read the question stem only. Identify the question type (Value or Yes/No).
  2. Identify any constraints in the stem — "x is a positive integer", "n is non-zero", etc. Carry these into both statements.
  3. Test Statement 1 alone. Try to construct two scenarios that satisfy Statement 1 but give different answers. If you can, Statement 1 is insufficient.
  4. Test Statement 2 alone. Same drill. Reset — pretend Statement 1 never existed.
  5. If both fail individually, combine. Now use both. Try again to construct two scenarios that satisfy both.
  6. If you cannot find conflicting scenarios with both statements together, the answer is C. Otherwise E.
  7. Pick the letter and move on. Do not go back to compute the actual value.

The four DS traps that break everyone

Trap · the C-trap

The "C-trap" is the most common DS error. Students see two statements that look like they need to be combined, automatically pick C, and miss the question. The fix: always test each statement alone, even when they look incomplete. Often one of them is sufficient by itself.

Trap · forgetting zero

"x ≠ 0" is rarely stated explicitly. If a statement says "x² = 4", remember it could be x = 2 or x = −2. If a statement says "xy > 0", both could be positive or both negative. The Zero Trap (Ch. 01) lives here too: if x = 0 is allowed, many product / divisibility statements collapse. See the Zero Trap appendix entry.

Trap · fractions and negatives in the variable's domain

If the stem does not say "integer", the variable can be a fraction or negative. "Is x² > x?" has different answers for x = 2 (yes), x = 0.5 (no), x = −1 (yes). Always test all three regions — positive integer, fraction in (0,1), negative — when the stem permits.

Trap · the "obvious answer" being a question rephrase

Some statements are sneaky restatements of the question. "Is x > y?" with Statement 1 "x − y > 0" is sufficient — they are the same. Recognize equivalence; do not mark insufficient just because the wording is different.

Worked examples

Worked Example · 01 · Value question

What is the value of x?
(1) 2x + 4 = 14
(2) x²= 25

  1. Question type: Value.
  2. Statement 1 alone: 2x = 10, so x = 5. Single value → sufficient. (A or D)
  3. Statement 2 alone: x = 5 or x = −5. Two values → insufficient.
  4. Answer: A.

Answer: A. Statement 1 alone is sufficient; Statement 2 is not.

Worked Example · 02 · Yes/No question with the C-trap

Is x > 0?
(1) x²> 0
(2) x³> 0

  1. Question type: Yes/No.
  2. Statement 1: x²> 0 means x ≠ 0. x could be positive or negative. Mixed → insufficient.
  3. Statement 2: x³> 0 → x is positive (cube preserves sign). Sufficient.
  4. Answer: B. Common C-trap: pairing two statements that look "complementary" when one alone already settles it.

Answer: B.

Worked Example · 03 · Combined sufficiency

If x and y are integers, what is the value of x + y?
(1) x − y = 4
(2) x²− y²= 28

  1. Statement 1 alone: many possibilities. x = 5, y = 1 gives x + y = 6; x = 6, y = 2 gives x + y = 8. Insufficient.
  2. Statement 2 alone: many factor pairs. (x+y)(x−y) = 28. x+y could be 14, 7, 28, etc. Insufficient.
  3. Combined: (x+y)(x−y) = 28 and x − y = 4. So x + y = 28 / 4 = 7. Single value → sufficient.
  4. Answer: C.

Answer: C.

Explanations · with structural reasoning

Explanation · 01↑ back to Q01
Answer · C

Statement 1: n²= 49 → n = 7 or n = −7. Two values → insufficient. Statement 2: n > 0 alone tells you nothing about the magnitude. Combined: n = 7. C.

Explanation · 02↑ back to Q02
Answer · C

Yes/No question.

Statement 1: x²= 16 → x = 4 (positive: yes) or x = −4 (positive: no). Mixed answers → insufficient.

Statement 2: x > −4. This includes x = 0 (not positive: no), x = 1 (positive: yes), and many other values. Mixed answers → insufficient.

Combined: From Statement 1, x ∈ {4, −4}. Statement 2 rules out −4 (since −4 is not strictly greater than −4). The only surviving value is x = 4, which is positive. Consistent yes → sufficient.

Answer: C. Always list the combined-statement candidates explicitly before declaring E.

Explanation · 03↑ back to Q03
Answer · C

Statement 1: factor pairs of 12 with both positive integers are (1,12), (2,6), (3,4). Three different sums (13, 8, 7). Insufficient. Statement 2: a − b = 1 alone has infinite solutions. Insufficient. Combined: among the factor pairs of 12, only (4, 3) has difference 1. So a + b = 7. C.

Explanation · 04↑ back to Q04
Answer · A

Statement 1: smallest prime greater than 30 is 31. Single value → sufficient. Statement 2: x between 30 and 40 has many possibilities. Insufficient. Answer A.

Explanation · 05↑ back to Q05
Answer · C

Yes/No. Statement 1: k divisible by 3. k = 3 (not div 6), k = 6 (yes). Mixed → insufficient. Statement 2: k divisible by 4. k = 4 (no), k = 12 (yes). Insufficient. Combined: divisible by both 3 and 4 means divisible by 12, hence by 6. Sufficient. Answer C.

Answer · C

Statement 1: x = |x| means x ≥ 0. Many values → insufficient. Statement 2: x²= 16 → x ∈ {4, −4}. Insufficient. Combined: x ≥ 0 rules out −4, leaving x = 4. Sufficient. C.
