Reading Comprehension

Why archetypes are the unlock

Most students read RC passages as if they were studying them. They are not. You will never see the same passage twice. The point is not to learn the passage; the point is to extract its structural skeleton — what each paragraph does, what the author thinks, what is asserted versus argued — and use that skeleton to answer questions in seconds.

Every official RC passage on the Focus Edition fits one of six archetypes. Once you can name the archetype, you can predict the question types and the trap answers before you read them.

The four-step RC procedure

  • 1. Identify the archetype from the first sentence and the topic phrase
  • 2. Map each paragraph by purpose, not content (claim, evidence, counter, qualification)
  • 3. Articulate the author's stance — agree, disagree, neutral, mixed
  • 4. Answer questions by returning to the map, not by re-reading

The six passage archetypes

Recognize on sight

  • A · Phenomenon & Explanation — describes a phenomenon, then offers one or more explanations. Common in science passages.
  • B · Old View vs. New View — the traditional understanding, then a revised theory that challenges it. Common in social science and history.
  • C · Argument & Counter-Argument — author advances a claim, raises objections, then defends. The author's stance is explicit.
  • D · Comparison / Two-Concept — compares two systems, theories, methods, or eras. Watch for similarities and differences.
  • E · Problem & Proposed Solution — defines a problem, evaluates one or more solutions. Common in business / economics.
  • F · Survey / Catalog — summarizes a body of research, no strong author position. The neutral archetype.
ELI5 · two simple examples

Example 1 · books on a shelf. Imagine a library where every book is one of six types: mystery, biography, cookbook, fairy tale, science textbook, or comic. The moment you open one, you know roughly what to expect — the structure is built into the genre. RC passages are the same. Six genres. Once you spot the genre, you read with the right expectations.

Example 2 · TV shows. Sitcoms have a setup, a conflict, and a resolution in 22 minutes. Crime dramas have a body in the first scene and a confession in the last. You watch each kind differently because you know the shape. RC passages have shapes too. Reading every passage with the same eye is like watching every TV show with the same expectations — you miss what matters.

The mapping technique — read for purpose, not content

The single biggest mistake students make in RC is trying to memorize content. The passage is not a textbook — you cannot learn the science of polymer self-assembly in 90 seconds. What you can do is map the structure of the argument: which paragraph introduces, which supports, which qualifies, which concludes.

Expert · the paragraph-purpose tags

Tag each paragraph mentally as you read:

  1. Set-up — introduces the topic, names the phenomenon or debate
  2. Claim — author's or someone's position
  3. Evidence — data, study, example supporting the claim
  4. Counter — objection or alternative view
  5. Qualification — narrows or limits the claim
  6. Synthesis / Conclusion — author's final stance

By the end of a passage, your mental map should look like: "P1 = Set-up. P2 = Claim + Evidence. P3 = Counter. P4 = Qualified Conclusion." Once you have that, you can return to any specific paragraph in seconds when a question demands it.

ELI5 · two simple examples

Example 1 · the recipe vs. the dish. When you cook, you do not memorize the recipe word for word. You glance to see "step 3 is the saute, step 4 is the simmer." Same with RC: you do not memorize the passage; you glance to know "P3 is the counter-argument, P4 is the conclusion." The map is enough.

Example 2 · the museum guide. A museum has six rooms, each with a theme. The guide does not memorize every painting — she knows "Room 3 is impressionism, Room 5 is modern." Then when a tourist asks "where's the Monet?" she walks straight to Room 3. RC works the same. Map first, navigate second.

The seven RC question types

The patterns and what they reward

  • Main Idea / Primary Purpose → rewards your map. Stays general; ignores supporting detail.
  • Specific Detail → rewards quick navigation. Returns to a single sentence in the passage.
  • Inference → rewards tight reading. The right answer is implied; close paraphrase is wrong.
  • Function / Purpose of a sentence or paragraph → rewards your tags. Why is this sentence here?
  • Tone / Attitude → rewards reading the verbs and adjectives the author chose. Word-for-word matters.
  • Logical Structure → rewards your map directly. What is the relationship between paragraphs?
  • Strengthen / Weaken (rare in RC) → same logic as Critical Reasoning, applied to a passage claim.
Trap · the "outside scope" answer

The most common wrong answer in RC is the choice that contains a true-sounding fact not actually supported by the passage. The right answer is only what the passage says or directly implies. If a choice introduces information the passage never mentions — even if you know it is true in real life — eliminate. See Outside-Scope Trap.

Pacing & pre-reading

Expert · the time budget

For a typical 350-word passage with three or four questions:

First read of passage:    2:00 to 2:30
Per question (after read): 0:45 to 1:15
Total per passage set:     ≈ 5 to 7 minutes

Read once, carefully, building the map. Then answer all questions without re-reading except to verify a specific detail. If you find yourself re-reading the whole passage, your first read failed — keep moving and learn from the miss in the error log.

Worked example — mapping a passage in real time

Worked Example · 01 · Map a short passage

Read this passage and identify the archetype, then tag each paragraph.

[P1] For decades, biologists believed that the rapid color changes of certain octopus species served primarily as camouflage. Recent research, however, has cast doubt on this interpretation. [P2] Studies of Octopus cyanea in the wild show that color shifts often occur when the animal is alone, well-hidden, and in no apparent danger from predators. [P3] Some biologists now propose that color shifts function as a form of communication, possibly between individuals of the same species. [P4] Yet skeptics note that octopuses are largely solitary creatures, raising the question of with whom they would communicate. The matter remains unresolved.

  1. Archetype: B · Old View vs. New View. The first sentence states the old view; "however" signals the pivot.
  2. P1 = Set-up + old view. P2 = Evidence challenging old view. P3 = New view (proposed). P4 = Counter to new view + qualified conclusion.
  3. Author's stance: neutral / skeptical of both. Notice "remains unresolved".

A clean four-tag map gives you the answer to almost every primary-purpose, function, and inference question without re-reading.

Practice set · 2 passages, 10 questions

Passage A

Until the 1980s, most economic historians treated the Industrial Revolution in Britain as the consequence of a unique convergence of factors: abundant coal, navigable rivers, and a culture of empirical inquiry. More recent scholarship, drawing on comparative data from the Netherlands and northern Italy, has challenged this view. These regions, the new historians argue, possessed similar institutional sophistication and capital markets, yet did not industrialize at the same pace. The decisive factor, they propose, was the relative cost of labor: high wages in Britain made labor-saving machinery economically attractive in a way it was not elsewhere. Critics counter that this account underweights cultural and institutional differences — particularly Britain's patent system, which incentivized inventors in ways that had no clear parallel on the Continent. Some recent work attempts to synthesize the two views, treating high wages as a necessary but not sufficient condition for industrialization, with patent-driven innovation as the catalyst that transformed wage pressure into mechanical progress.

The primary purpose of the passage is to:

  1. argue that high wages were the sole cause of British industrialization
  2. describe a debate among historians and the synthesis attempts to reconcile competing views
  3. refute the patent-system theory of industrialization
  4. compare British and Dutch economic institutions
  5. establish that Britain industrialized faster than the Netherlands
Solution →

According to the passage, the older view of British industrialization emphasized:

  1. high relative wages
  2. a unique combination of resources, geography, and intellectual culture
  3. Britain's patent system
  4. colonial trade networks
  5. weak institutional development on the Continent
Solution →

Which of the following can be most reasonably inferred from the passage?

  1. The Netherlands had lower wages than Britain during the relevant period.
  2. The patent system in Britain was the only factor distinguishing it from northern Italy.
  3. High wages made labor-saving machinery more attractive in Britain than in regions with comparable institutions.
  4. Northern Italy never industrialized.
  5. Empirical inquiry was the most important cultural factor.
Solution →

The author's attitude toward the synthesis view described at the end of the passage is best described as:

  1. strong endorsement
  2. open skepticism
  3. measured presentation, without explicit endorsement or rejection
  4. dismissive
  5. confused
Solution →

The passage suggests that the new historians would most likely respond to the claim that "Britain's coal endowment was the principal cause of its industrialization" by:

  1. agreeing entirely
  2. arguing that other regions with comparable resources did not industrialize at the same pace
  3. noting that coal was unimportant
  4. citing patent records
  5. blaming cultural differences
Solution →

Passage B

A long-standing puzzle in cell biology is how individual cells maintain stable shapes despite the constant turnover of their internal protein scaffolding. One leading explanation, the "treadmill model", proposes that protein filaments grow at one end while shrinking at the other, producing a steady-state structure even as molecules cycle through. While elegant, this model has trouble accounting for the speed with which certain cells, such as those at the leading edge of a migrating amoeba, can reorganize their shape — sometimes within seconds. Critics argue that pure treadmilling cannot accelerate or decelerate fast enough. A revised proposal, the "regulated assembly model", suggests that cells maintain a reservoir of unbound protein subunits and recruit them rapidly when needed, allowing for sudden, localized restructuring. Recent imaging studies appear to support the regulated assembly model in fast-moving cell types, though some researchers caution that treadmilling and regulated assembly may operate simultaneously in different parts of the same cell.

The passage primarily serves to:

  1. establish that the treadmill model is incorrect
  2. introduce two models of cellular structure maintenance and discuss their relative merits
  3. describe the experimental methods of recent imaging studies
  4. argue that fast-moving cells are biologically unique
  5. propose a new model of cell migration
Solution →

According to the passage, a primary limitation of the treadmill model is that:

  1. it does not account for protein turnover
  2. it cannot explain the rapid shape changes observed in some cells
  3. it assumes cells have unlimited protein reservoirs
  4. it has been disproved by imaging studies
  5. it ignores the role of regulated assembly
Solution →

It can be inferred from the passage that the regulated assembly model is most likely to gain support from observations of:

  1. cells that maintain stable shapes over long periods
  2. cells that undergo sudden, localized changes in shape
  3. cells that are not motile
  4. cells in which protein turnover does not occur
  5. plant cells with rigid cell walls
Solution →

The phrase "though some researchers caution that treadmilling and regulated assembly may operate simultaneously" primarily serves to:

  1. argue that the regulated assembly model is incorrect
  2. qualify the apparent victory of one model over the other
  3. introduce a third model of cell structure
  4. question the imaging studies
  5. describe the limits of recent technology
Solution →

Which of the following best describes the structural relationship between the third sentence ("While elegant…") and the fourth sentence ("Critics argue…")?

  1. The fourth sentence provides evidence for the claim in the third.
  2. The fourth sentence introduces a counter-claim that contradicts the third.
  3. The fourth sentence further specifies the limitation introduced in the third.
  4. The fourth sentence rejects the treadmill model entirely.
  5. The fourth sentence is unrelated to the third.
Solution →

The passage describes an old view, a new view, a counter, and a synthesis. B captures this. A is too strong (the passage does not endorse the wage view alone). C is wrong direction. D is too narrow. E is a side fact, not the primary purpose.

Explanation · 02↑ back to Q02
Answer · B

The passage opens with "abundant coal, navigable rivers, and a culture of empirical inquiry" — that is the unique resources/geography/culture combo. B paraphrases it. A is the new view; C, D, E are unsupported.

Explanation · 03↑ back to Q03
Answer · C

The passage explicitly says high wages made labor-saving machinery economically attractive in Britain in a way it was not elsewhere — exactly C. A is more specific than the passage warrants (we don't know the Netherlands' wages directly, only the implication). B is too strong ("the only factor"). D is unsupported. E is the old view, not an inference.

Answer · C

The author describes the synthesis as "some recent work attempts to synthesize" without using endorsement language. The author neither praises nor dismisses. C is the calibrated answer.

The new historians' core move was to compare Britain with regions that had similar institutional setups but did not industrialize. They would apply the same comparative logic to coal: other regions had coal too. B captures this exactly.

The passage introduces both models, presents evidence for each, and notes a possible synthesis at the end. B is the only choice that captures the comparative-evaluation purpose.
