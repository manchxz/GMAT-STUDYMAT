Word-Problem Deconstruction

A word problem is a translation exercise dressed as a math question. Translate first, compute last. The story is the trap; the structure is the answer.

Word problems are translation problems

The hardest thing about a word problem is not the math. It is the fact that a math equation has been hidden inside a sentence about trains, painters, salt water, or cousins. Solve the translation step and the algebra is trivial. Skip the translation step and the algebra is impossible.

This chapter gives you a fixed translation alphabet (English phrase → math symbol) and three structural templates (Rate, Work, Mixture) that absorb roughly 90% of all GMAT word problems. You will leave with a reflex: read the problem, identify the template, fill in the slots, compute.

The four-step word-problem process

  • 1. Identify the template → rate, work, mixture, age, or "general translation".
  • 2. Define variables explicitly → in writing, with units. r = miles per hour, not just "r".
  • 3. Translate each sentence into one equation → one English clause = one math statement.
  • 4. Solve, then verify the answer matches the question asked.

The translation alphabet

Every recurring English phrase on the GMAT has a math equivalent. Here are the ones to memorize.

English → Math dictionary

  • "is", "equals", "the same as" → =
  • "of" → × (e.g. "20% of x" = 0.2 × x)
  • "per" → ÷ (e.g. "miles per hour" = miles ÷ hours)
  • "more than", "greater than" → +
  • "less than", "fewer than" → − (and reverse the order: "5 less than x" = x − 5, not 5 − x)
  • "twice", "double" → 2 ×
  • "a number" → an unknown variable
  • "how many … did each …" → ratio / division
ELI5 · two simple examples

Example 1 · the translation app. Imagine an app that translates Spanish into English word-by-word. Word problems work the same way — every word in the English sentence has a math equivalent. "Three more than twice a number" becomes 2x + 3. The app does not jump ahead to the meaning; it goes word by word.

Example 2 · cooking from a recipe in another language. If a recipe is written in French and you do not speak French, you cannot start cooking. You first translate ingredients and steps into English. Only then do you cook. Word problems are the same: read, translate every clause to math, then "cook" the equation.

Trap · "less than" reverses

"5 less than x" is x − 5, not 5 − x. This single trap accounts for an embarrassing number of missed Quant problems. Read these phrases twice. The number after "less than" is the one you subtract from.

Rate · time · distance — one formula, infinite disguises

Every motion problem on the GMAT reduces to one formula: distance = rate × time. If you know two of the three, you can compute the third. The questions get harder by varying which two are given and by adding more travelers.

The R · T · D toolbox

  • d = r × t the master equation
  • r = d / t
  • t = d / r
  • Always check units. "60 mph for 30 minutes" means r = 60, t = 0.5 (hours), so d = 30. Mixed units cause more wrong answers than any other single thing.
ELI5 · two simple examples

Example 1 · driving to a friend's house. If you drive 50 miles per hour for 2 hours, you have gone 100 miles. That is just 50 × 2 = 100. The "rate" is how fast, the "time" is how long, and the "distance" is how far you got. It is one of the most natural multiplications you do every day without thinking of it as algebra.

Example 2 · pouring water from a hose. A hose pours water at 4 liters per minute. After 6 minutes, it has poured 24 liters. That is the same formula in a different costume — rate × time = total. Whether the units are miles or liters, the structure is identical.

Expert · the R-T-D table layout

For any motion problem with multiple travelers or legs, draw a table:

            Rate  ×  Time  =  Distance
Traveler A  ──    ──        ──
Traveler B  ──    ──        ──

Fill in what the problem gives. Then use the relationship between the rows (same distance, same time, sum of distances, etc.) to solve for the unknown.

Combined motion — toward, apart, catch-up

The hardest motion problems on the GMAT involve two travelers. There are three structural cases, and all three reduce to one principle: combine the rates correctly.

The three cases

  • Toward each other → combined rate = r₁ + r₂. Time to meet = (initial distance) / (combined rate).
  • Moving apart → same combined rate. Distance apart = (combined rate) × time.
  • Same direction (catch-up) → combined rate = r_fast − r_slow. Time to catch = (head start) / (rate gap).
ELI5 · two simple examples

Example 1 · two ants on a stick. Two ants are at opposite ends of a 12-inch stick, walking toward each other. One walks 2 inches per second, the other walks 1 inch per second. Together they cover 3 inches per second. The 12 inches will be closed in 12 ÷ 3 = 4 seconds. That is "combined rate" in plain English.

Example 2 · the older sibling catching up. Your little sister has a 10-step head start in a hallway race. You walk 3 steps per second, she walks 1 step per second. Each second, you gain 2 steps on her. To close 10 steps takes 10 ÷ 2 = 5 seconds. That is the catch-up case: only the rate difference matters.

Work problems — the rate is "fraction of a job per unit time"

Work problems are motion problems wearing a different costume. Replace "distance" with "job" and "rate" is now "fraction of the job done per unit time".

The work formula

  • rate × time = work done (work is usually 1 = "one whole job")
  • If A finishes in a hours, A's rate = 1/a jobs per hour
  • Combined rate = sum of individual rates when workers are doing the same job together
  • Combined time = 1 / (combined rate)
ELI5 · two simple examples

Example 1 · painting a fence. If Anna can paint a fence in 4 hours alone, in one hour she paints 1/4 of the fence. If Ben can paint it in 6 hours, in one hour he paints 1/6. Together, in one hour, they paint 1/4 + 1/6 = 5/12 of the fence. The whole fence takes them 12/5 = 2.4 hours together.

Example 2 · two kids cleaning a room. One kid can clean a room in 30 minutes alone, so per minute he cleans 1/30 of the room. The other kid takes 60 minutes alone, so she cleans 1/60 per minute. Together: 1/30 + 1/60 = 3/60 = 1/20 of the room per minute. Total time together: 20 minutes — faster than either alone.

Expert · the structural shortcut

For two workers A and B with individual times a and b, the combined time is:

combined time = (a × b) / (a + b)

Memorize this. It is just a clean rearrangement of 1/a + 1/b = 1/T. Saves the fraction arithmetic on every two-worker problem.

Mixtures & weighted averages — the volume × concentration trick

Mixture problems are the GMAT's favorite Data Insights crossover. The key concept is that, for any mixed solution, the amount of each component is conserved.

The mixture identity

  • amount of substance = volume × concentration
  • Mixing two solutions: total substance = sum of individual substances; total volume = sum of individual volumes; new concentration = total substance / total volume
  • Adding pure substance: treat it as a "100% concentration" addition
  • Evaporating water: substance stays, volume decreases — concentration goes up
ELI5 · two simple examples

Example 1 · mixing two glasses of juice. Glass A is 200 mL of 30% juice (so 60 mL of pure juice and 140 mL of water). Glass B is 100 mL of 60% juice (60 mL of juice, 40 mL of water). Pour them together: 300 mL total, 120 mL of juice, so 120/300 = 40%. The juice does not disappear during pouring — it just gets redistributed.

Example 2 · adding sugar to coffee. A cup of coffee is 200 mL with 5% sugar (10 mL of sugar). You stir in 5 mL of pure sugar. Now you have 205 mL total with 15 mL of sugar, so concentration is 15/205 ≈ 7.3%. The sugar count is what stays trackable; the percentage is just a ratio at the end.

Ages & relative values — the "time-shift" trick

Age problems look like a separate category but they are really one trick: everyone ages by the same amount each year. If A is 5 years older than B today, A is 5 years older than B 100 years from now and 100 years ago.

ELI5 · two simple examples

Example 1 · siblings on a road trip. You are 3 years older than your sister today. Five years from now, you will still be 3 years older. Twenty years from now, still 3 years older. Age gaps are constant. Most age problems live on this single fact.

Example 2 · two trees in a yard. An oak was planted 5 years before a maple. Today, the oak is 5 years older than the maple. In 50 years, the oak will be 55 and the maple will be 50 — still 5 apart. The "gap" is the conserved quantity.

Worked examples

Worked Example · 01 · Standard motion

A car drives 240 miles in 4 hours. What is its average speed in miles per hour?

  1. Identify: rate · time · distance template.
  2. Apply r = d / t.
  3. Compute: 240 / 4 = 60 mph.

Answer: 60 mph.

Worked Example · 02 · Combined work

Pipe A fills a tank in 6 hours. Pipe B fills the same tank in 12 hours. How long does it take both pipes together to fill the tank?

  1. Use the combined-time shortcut: T = (a × b) / (a + b).
  2. Plug in: (6 × 12) / (6 + 12) = 72 / 18 = 4.

Answer: 4 hours.

Worked Example · 03 · Mixture by conservation

A solution of 200 mL is 25% salt. How much pure water must be added to make it 20% salt?

  1. Identify: salt is conserved; water is added.
  2. Salt before = 200 × 0.25 = 50 mL. Salt after = still 50 mL.
  3. Let new total volume be V. Then 50 / V = 0.20, so V = 250 mL.
  4. Water added = 250 − 200 = 50 mL.

Answer: 50 mL.

Explanations

Explanation · 01↑ back to Q01
Answer · C · 9 hours

Speed = 360 / 6 = 60 mph. Time for 540 miles = 540 / 60 = 9 hours.

Explanation · 02↑ back to Q02
Answer · B · 4.8 hours

Combined-time shortcut: T = (8 × 12) / (8 + 12) = 96 / 20 = 4.8 hours.

Explanation · 03↑ back to Q03
Answer · E · 360 miles

Opposite directions → combined rate = 50 + 70 = 120 mph. Distance apart after 3 hours = 120 × 3 = 360 miles.

Explanation · 04↑ back to Q04
Answer · C · 200 mL

Acid is conserved. Acid amount = 600 × 0.4 = 240 mL. Let new volume = V. Then 240 / V = 0.30, so V = 800. Water added = 800 − 600 = 200 mL.

Explanation · 05↑ back to Q05
Answer · D · 20

Let Ben be b now, Anna 2b. In 6 years: (b + 6) + (2b + 6) = 42 → 3b + 12 = 42 → b = 10. Anna is 2 × 10 = 20.

Explanation · 06↑ back to Q06
Answer · C · 10

Downstream rate = 24 / 2 = 12 mph. Upstream rate = 24 / 3 = 8 mph. Boat in still water is the average of the two: (12 + 8) / 2 = 10 mph.

Why? Downstream rate = boat + current; upstream rate = boat − current. Their average cancels the current.

Explanation · 07↑ back to Q07
Answer · C · 60

If 30% × N = 24, then N = 80. So 75% × 80 = 60.

Faster: 75% / 30% = 2.5, so the answer is 24 × 2.5 = 60.

Explanation · 08↑ back to Q08
Answer · D · 3 hours

Toward each other → combined rate = 40 + 60 = 100 mph. Time = 300 / 100 = 3 hours.

Explanation · 09↑ back to Q09
Answer · D · 20

Let x = lb of Coffee X, so 30 − x = lb of Y. Total cost equation:

8x + 14(30 − x) = 10 × 30
8x + 420 − 14x = 300
−6x = −120
x = 20

Sanity check: 20 lb at $8 = $160; 10 lb at $14 = $140; total = $300; 30 lb at $10 = $300. ✓

Explanation · 10↑ back to Q10
Answer · C · 3:00 PM

Catch-up problem. By 11:00 AM (when Car B starts), Car A has been driving for 2 hours, so the head start is 50 × 2 = 100 miles. The rate gap is 75 − 50 = 25 mph. Time for B to close the gap: 100 / 25 = 4 hours after B starts.

11:00 AM + 4 hours = 3:00 PM.

Verification: at 3:00 PM, Car A has driven 50 × 6 = 300 miles; Car B has driven 75 × 4 = 300 miles. Same distance → they meet. ✓
