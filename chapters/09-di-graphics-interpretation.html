Graphics Interpretation

Read the axes before the bars. Read the units before the numbers. Read the scale before the trend. Skip these in order and the chart will lie to you.

What Graphics Interpretation actually is

A Graphics Interpretation question gives you one chart — bar, scatter, line, pie, or occasionally a network — and asks you to fill in two drop-down menus to complete a sentence based on the chart. Each drop-down has 4 to 7 options. The skill being tested is whether you can extract the right number from the right place under time pressure.

The format rewards careful reading and punishes careless reading. The single most common mistake is answering before identifying the units on the axes — the second-most-common is answering based on the visual length of a bar without checking the y-axis scale.

The GI core principles

  • 1. Read both axes (or the legend) before reading any data point.
  • 2. Note the units (millions, percentages, raw counts).
  • 3. Note the scale (does the y-axis start at 0 or 100?).
  • 4. Then answer the question.
  • Time budget · 2:30 to 3:00 per chart.

The five chart types you will see

Each chart, what it is best at

  • Bar chart compares discrete categories. Good for "which is largest", "ranking".
  • Line chart shows change over time. Good for trends, slopes, peaks.
  • Scatter plot shows relationship between two variables. Good for correlation.
  • Pie chart shows parts of a whole. Good for share / proportion.
  • Network / org chart shows relationships. Good for connection / hierarchy.
ELI5 · two simple examples

Example 1 · the school cafeteria. If the lunch lady wants to compare how much food each grade ate, she draws a bar chart. If she wants to see how lunch attendance changed over the school year, she draws a line chart. If she wants to see whether kids who eat lunch also do well on tests, she draws a scatter plot. Different stories want different chart shapes.

Example 2 · the family photo album. A pie chart is "how many photos came from each family member" (parts of a whole). A bar chart is "how many photos each year" (discrete categories). A line chart is "photos per month over time" (a trend). The chart type tells you what kind of question is being asked.

The read-the-axes procedure

Expert · the 30-second pre-read
  1. Title — what is being shown overall?
  2. X-axis — what is the variable? In what units?
  3. Y-axis — what is the variable? In what units? Does the scale start at 0?
  4. Legend — what does each color, shape, or pattern represent?
  5. One reference point — pick a single bar or dot and confirm you can read it correctly.

Only after this 30-second pre-read should you look at the question.

Trap · truncated y-axis

If a bar chart's y-axis starts at, say, 80 instead of 0, the visual difference between bars looks much larger than the actual numeric difference. Do not "eyeball" the difference; read the values. See Truncated-Axis Trap.

The drop-down completion strategy

ELI5 · two simple examples

Example 1 · fill in the blank with a word. Imagine a teacher gives you the sentence "The fastest animal in this list is ____" and a list of animals. You do not guess — you check the list. GI drop-downs work the same: do not pick the option that "feels biggest", read the chart and pick the option that matches.

Example 2 · the multiple-choice grocery list. "I bought ____ apples and ____ oranges" with options 3, 5, 7, 10. The receipt is in your hand. You do not guess — you read the receipt. The chart is your receipt.

Expert · the two-blank routine
  1. Read the entire sentence first — both blanks. Understand what the sentence is claiming.
  2. Map each blank to a specific chart read. "Approximately X" usually means a single value. "Increased by Y%" means a comparison.
  3. Compute / read in the order that locks faster blanks first. If one blank is easy, fill it; the other may then be obvious.
  4. For percentage-change blanks, use rough math. The choices are spaced wide enough that (new − old) / old need only be approximate.

Graphics-specific traps

Trap · "share" vs. "absolute"

A pie chart shows shares — percentages of a whole. A bar chart can show either, depending on whether the bars sum to 100% or to absolute counts. Mixing up "share" and "count" is a constant trap. Always check the y-axis label.

Trap · double y-axis

Some charts have two y-axes — one on the left, one on the right — with different scales. The bar uses one axis, the line uses the other. If you read both off the same scale, you will be wrong.

Trap · selective time window

A line chart that starts in 2018 may hide a bigger trend that began in 2010. The chart shows only what is shown. If the question asks about a longer period, the answer is "cannot be determined" — not "extrapolate".

Worked example · live chart

Annual Revenue by Region ($ millions)
Worked Example · 01 · Drop-down completion

Complete the sentence based on the chart above:

The region with the highest revenue is East, and its revenue is approximately 100% greater than that of the South region.
  1. First blank: read the bar chart. East = 200, the tallest bar.
  2. Second blank: percentage difference between East ($200M) and South ($100M). (200 − 100) / 100 = 100%. East is 100% greater than South.

Answer: East, 100%.

Explanations

Explanation · 01↑ back to Q01
Answer · C · 8%

Q4: Aurora ≈ $70M, Borealis ≈ $65M. Difference: $5M. Percentage: 5 / 65 ≈ 7.7%. Closest choice: 8%.

Explanation · 02↑ back to Q02
Answer · A · Q1

Gaps (Borealis − Aurora) by quarter: Q1: 50 − 30 = 20; Q2: 55 − 40 = 15; Q3: 60 − 50 = 10; Q4: 65 − 70 = −5 (Aurora ahead). Largest gap with Borealis ahead is Q1.

Explanation · 03↑ back to Q03
Answer · C · $40M

Aurora Q1 ≈ $30M, Q4 ≈ $70M. Growth = $40M.

Explanation · 04↑ back to Q04
Answer · D · Q4

From Q1 to Q3, Borealis was ahead. In Q4, Aurora overtook Borealis ($70M vs. $65M).

Explanation · 05↑ back to Q05
Answer · C · $190M

Aurora: 30 + 40 + 50 + 70 = 190.

Explanation · 06↑ back to Q06
Answer · A · Strong positive correlation

The points trend upward and to the right in a tight band — higher ad spend, higher unit sales. That is the textbook description of a strong positive correlation.

Explanation · 07↑ back to Q07
Answer · B · 150

Lowest ad spend is around $5K. Reading the y-axis gives ≈ 150 unit sales.

Explanation · 08↑ back to Q08
Answer · D · 400

Highest ad spend is around $38K. Reading the y-axis gives ≈ 380–400 unit sales. Closest choice: 400.

Explanation · 09↑ back to Q09
Answer · C · 270

Interpolate. At $20K, sales ≈ 260. At $25K, sales ≈ 280–300. The closest choice is 270.

Explanation · 10↑ back to Q10
Answer · B

The chart shows correlation, not causation. B captures both the trend and the limitation. A overstates ("always causes proportional increases"). C contradicts the visible trend. D adds information not in the chart. E overstates exactness.

Cross-reference: see Ch. 04 · CR · Correlation = Causation flaw. The same pattern appears in DI charts.
