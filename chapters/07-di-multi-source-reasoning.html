Multi-Source Reasoning

Three tabs. Three questions. Each answer hides at the intersection of two or more tabs. Read all three first; answer none of them yet.

What Multi-Source Reasoning actually is

An MSR question gives you three tabs of information — usually some combination of an email thread, a spreadsheet, a chart, a memo, or a research summary — and asks three questions about the combined contents. The trap is that no single tab contains the answer to any of the three questions. Each answer requires triangulating across at least two tabs.

Students fail MSR by reading one tab at a time, answering as they go. By the time they reach Tab 3, they have already misanswered Q1 because Q1 quietly required Tab 3. Read all three tabs first. Build a mental index of "Tab A contains X, Tab B contains Y, Tab C contains Z". Then answer all three questions.

The MSR core principles

  • Read all three tabs before answering any question.
  • Build a quick "what-lives-where" index as you read.
  • Triangulate by default. Single-source answers are usually traps.
  • Distinguish facts from opinions in each tab. Especially when emails or memos are involved.
  • Time budget · 6–7 minutes total for the set.

Tab discipline — read each tab once, well

ELI5 · two simple examples

Example 1 · the three-page menu. Imagine a restaurant with three menu pages: appetizers, mains, desserts. You are placing one combined order. You would never order an appetizer before glancing at the desserts to see if you have room. MSR works the same way — you must scan all three "menus" before deciding what to "order".

Example 2 · piecing together a story from three letters. You receive three letters from three different family members about a wedding. None of them tells the whole story. One mentions the date, another the venue, the third a guest list. To answer "who is invited and where is it on Saturday?", you have to read all three and stitch the answer together.

Expert · the per-tab read

For each tab, in 30–45 seconds:

  1. Identify the source type — email? memo? table? chart? Each has different reliability characteristics.
  2. Note the entities — who or what is being discussed?
  3. Note the numbers — but do not memorize them. You will return.
  4. Note any opinions, hedges, or conditional language — "we believe", "if X, then Y", "assuming…".

Source triangulation — the answer is at the intersection

The hardest MSR questions cannot be answered by any single tab. The question forces you to combine, for example, a sales figure in Tab A with a price in Tab B and a margin definition in Tab C. The "intersection" of all three is the answer.

ELI5 · two simple examples

Example 1 · the recipe with split ingredients. A recipe is divided across three index cards: card A has the dry ingredients, card B has the wet ingredients, card C has the steps. You cannot bake from any one card alone. You need all three. MSR is exactly this: each tab is one card.

Example 2 · a budget across receipts. You want to know how much you spent on lunch this week. The receipts are split among three pockets — one in your bag, one on your desk, one in your jacket. You have to gather all three before you can total. The "answer" is at the intersection of three pockets, not in any one.

The three MSR question types

What each type asks for

  • True / False / Cannot be determined — a series of statements; mark each. Usually three statements per question.
  • Single-best-answer multiple choice — five choices, find the one supported by the tabs.
  • Calculation — compute a value using data spread across tabs.
Trap · "cannot be determined" is a real answer

For T/F/CBD questions, "cannot be determined" is correct surprisingly often. If the tabs do not give you the data to mark a statement true or false, the right call is "cannot be determined" — not a guess in either direction.

When tabs conflict — choose the more authoritative source

Sometimes tabs seem to disagree. Maybe an email opinion contradicts a spreadsheet value. The rule: data > opinion. If a chart, table, or formal report disagrees with a person's email-stated opinion, side with the data unless the question is specifically asking about the opinion itself.

Trap · opinion-as-fact

An email may say "we expect sales to grow 20%". Even if the email is in a tab, the 20% is an expectation, not a fact. If a question asks "what was the actual sales growth?", look at the data tab, not the email. See Opinion-as-Fact Trap.

PDF layout directives — print fidelity for tab-based content

Expert · how the print stylesheet handles MSR

In the live HTML, MSR scenarios use clickable tabs (Tab 1, Tab 2, Tab 3). When the textbook is printed:

  1. The tab strip is hidden and all three tab contents are stacked vertically.
  2. Each tab is preceded by a tracked-out caps label ("Tab — Email Thread", "Tab — Sales Table", "Tab — Memo") so the reader can still identify which information came from which source.
  3. For dynamic datasets that cannot fit on paper, a QR code (rendered via the browser-friendly qrcode-svg library) is embedded next to the relevant tab, linking to the live data resource. In print, the QR code resolves to a static URL the reader can scan with a phone.

This approach lets the same source compile to a working web textbook and a printable book without changing content.

Worked example · live MSR scenario

Email from VP of Sales · Friday 4:12 PM

Team — Q3 numbers came in stronger than expected. Total unit sales for our flagship product were up year-over-year, and our Northeast region in particular had a standout quarter. We expect Q4 to grow another 10% if current marketing spend is maintained. — Priya

Q3 Unit Sales by Region (vs. Q3 prior year)

Region        Q3 (this yr)   Q3 (last yr)   Change
Northeast      14,200         11,800         +20%
Southeast       9,400          9,000          +4%
Midwest         8,200          8,500          −4%
West           12,300         12,000          +3%
Total          44,100         41,300          +7%

Memo · Marketing Department

Q3 marketing spend totaled $1.2M, of which 45% was allocated to the Northeast region. The Q4 plan reduces total spend to $1.0M, with the same 45% Northeast share, contingent on board approval.

Worked Example · 01 · Triangulate three tabs

Question: "If the board does not approve the Q4 marketing plan, the VP's projection of 10% Q4 growth would be:"

  1. The 10% Q4 growth projection is in Tab 1.
  2. The "if current marketing spend is maintained" condition is in Tab 1.
  3. Tab 3 reveals that the Q4 plan is contingent on board approval — and the spend is being reduced, not maintained.
  4. So if the board does not approve, the spend assumption fails entirely (the plan does not even take effect, but the new lower spend would not happen either; the prior spend continues — wait, re-read). Actually: if the plan is rejected, the new lower spend does not happen and the prior spend (presumably the $1.2M) might continue. The 10% projection would then be more, not less, defensible. The right framing depends on the answer choices.

Answer (typical wording): "based on an assumption (continued spend) that may or may not hold; the question's answer hinges on whether 'current spend' refers to the new plan's spend or the existing $1.2M." The structural takeaway: every MSR projection is conditional, and you must locate the condition before evaluating the projection.

Practice scenario · second set, 5 questions

Concept Note · "Verde Kitchen"

Verde Kitchen is a fast-casual restaurant concept centered on Mediterranean bowls. The owner is deciding between two locations: Downtown (high foot traffic, high rent) and Riverside (medium foot traffic, lower rent). The owner expects an average customer ticket of $14 and is targeting a 20% net profit margin in year one.

  4. $330,000
  5. $360,000

Which of the following can the owner most reasonably infer from the tabs?

  1. Downtown will have a higher net profit margin than Riverside.
  2. Riverside has lower fixed costs but a slightly higher per-visit ticket potential, which could offset the lower foot traffic.
  3. Riverside will have more weekly visitors than Downtown.
  4. Both locations will reach the 20% net profit margin target.
  5. Survey respondents at Riverside have eaten at more Mediterranean restaurants.
Solution →

Explanations

Explanation · 01↑ back to Q01
Answer · True

Tab 3 shows Design allocated $80,000, spent $82,000 — that is over by $2,000. True.

Explanation · 02↑ back to Q02
Answer · True

Tab 1 says the planned full release was February. Tab 2 shows it has been delayed to March. The combination confirms the statement.

Explanation · 03↑ back to Q03
Answer · False

Design has already exceeded its allocation by $2,000. So even if Engineering stays under budget, the project as a whole has already triggered an over-allocation event in Design — which would, under the brief's rule (Tab 1), require CFO approval. False.

Explanation · 04↑ back to Q04
Answer · D

Tab 2 says public beta is "delayed to Nov" and notes "pending eng[ineering]". Tab 2 also says Engineering is "2 weeks behind". The most likely cause is Engineering being behind schedule. The other choices are unsupported or unrelated.

Explanation · 05↑ back to Q05
Answer · C · 56%

Engineering allocated $260,000, spent $145,000. 145 / 260 ≈ 0.558, or about 56%.

Explanation · 06↑ back to Q06
Answer · True

Tab 2 shows Riverside willingness-to-pay at $15.00 vs. Downtown $13.50. True.

Explanation · 07↑ back to Q07
Answer · False

The owner's $14 expectation is above Downtown's $13.50 willingness-to-pay (so customers there might balk) and below Riverside's $15.00. The expectation is not consistent with both locations — it overestimates Downtown's appetite. False.

Explanation · 08↑ back to Q08
Answer · True

Sum the fixed-cost lines (rent + labor + other fixed):

Downtown:  180 + 260 + 40 = $480,000
Riverside:  96 + 230 + 35 = $361,000

$361K < $480K. True.

Explanation · 09↑ back to Q09
Answer · C · $300,000

20,000 × $15.00 = $300,000.

Explanation · 10↑ back to Q10
Answer · B

B is the only inference fully supported by all three tabs: Riverside has lower fixed costs (Tab 3) and a higher per-visit ticket potential (Tab 2), which could offset lower foot traffic (Tab 1). A and D require profit calculations that depend on customer counts not given. C confuses "would visit at least monthly" with actual visits. E is unsupported.
