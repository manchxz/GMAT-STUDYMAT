How to use this library

Each entry follows the same template:

  1. Name + where it appears. The name is the mental tag you should attach to every miss caused by this pattern.
  2. Pattern. The structural shape of the trap — what the question looks like.
  3. Example. A concrete instance, written like a real GMAT prompt.
  4. Defense. The exact countermove. Memorize the defense, not the example.

Before every full-length mock, scan the trap names. You do not need to re-read every entry. The mental tags are the value; the entries are the deep reference when a tag does not feel sharp.

Trap · 01 The Zero Trap

Quant · Number Properties · Data Sufficiency · Algebraic Logic
Pattern

A problem allows a variable to be zero, but the student silently assumes the variable is positive (or non-zero). Since zero behaves differently from every other number — it is even, it is a multiple of everything, it is neither positive nor negative — the assumption breaks the answer.

Example

"If k is a non-negative integer, what is the smallest value of k for which 200 × k is a perfect square?" The "obvious" answer is k = 2, since 200 × 2 = 400 = 20². But k = 0 works too — 200 × 0 = 0 = 0² — and 0 is smaller.

Defense

For every variable in the problem, ask: "Could this be zero?" If the constraint is "non-negative", yes. If "non-zero", no. If unstated, default to assuming yes and check whether the answer changes.

Cross-references: Ch. 01 · Parity & Sign; Ch. 06 · DS Traps.

Trap · 02 The Reverse Logic Trap

Critical Reasoning · Data Sufficiency · Algebraic Logic
Pattern

The author or the question reverses the direction of an inference. "A implies B" gets confused with "B implies A". In CR this shows up as reverse causation. In algebra this shows up as confusing necessary with sufficient conditions.

Example

CR: "Workers who use standing desks have less back pain. Therefore, standing desks reduce back pain." Reverse logic: maybe people who already had less back pain were more likely to choose standing desks (reverse causation).

Algebra: "If x > 5, then x > 0." True. The reverse, "If x > 0, then x > 5," is false. Confusing the two costs you DS questions.

Defense

Whenever you encounter "A implies B", ask out loud whether the reverse "B implies A" also holds. The answer is almost always no — and the test-writer is hoping you assume yes.

Cross-reference: Ch. 04 · CR · Reverse Causation.

Trap · 03 The Extreme Word Trap

Critical Reasoning · Reading Comprehension · Inference questions
Pattern

An answer choice contains an absolute, total, or extreme word: "always", "never", "all", "none", "the only", "must", "exactly", "uniquely". The passage rarely supports such absolutes. These choices look authoritative but usually overshoot.

Example

RC inference question. Choice: "The author believes that all economists prior to 1980 ignored institutional differences." But the passage said "most economic historians" — not "all". The choice is too strong. Eliminate.

Defense

Highlight every absolute word in every answer choice. For each, ask: "does the passage actually support this absolute?" If even one counterexample is allowed, the choice is wrong. Prefer answers with hedged language ("often", "tend to", "may", "in some cases") — they match the passage's typical caution.

Cross-reference: Ch. 05 · RC Question Types.

Trap · 04 The Compound Divisibility Trap

Number Properties · Data Sufficiency
Pattern

Divisibility by a composite number requires divisibility by each of its prime-power factors. Students check one factor and forget the others.

Example

"Is n divisible by 6?" Statement: "n is divisible by 3." This is insufficient because divisibility by 6 also requires divisibility by 2. n = 9 is divisible by 3 but not 6.

Defense

For divisibility by k, write k in prime-power form. 6 = 2 × 3; 12 = 2² × 3; 15 = 3 × 5; 72 = 2³ × 3². n is divisible by k if and only if n is divisible by every prime-power piece of k.

Cross-reference: Ch. 01 · Divisibility Rules.

Trap · 05 The Extraneous Solution Trap

Algebraic Logic · Equation Solving
Pattern

Squaring both sides of an equation can introduce solutions that did not satisfy the original. Multiplying both sides by an expression that could be zero can do the same.

Example

Solve √(x + 3) = x − 3. Square both sides: x + 3 = (x − 3)² = x² − 6x + 9, giving x² − 7x + 6 = 0, so x = 1 or x = 6. Plug back into the original: x = 6 works (√9 = 3 = 6 − 3) but x = 1 does not (√4 = 2 ≠ 1 − 3 = −2). x = 1 is extraneous.

Defense

After squaring or multiplying both sides, always plug each candidate back into the original equation. Discard any that do not check.

Cross-reference: Ch. 02 · Equations & Symmetry.

Trap · 06 The Outside-Scope Trap

Reading Comprehension · Critical Reasoning · MSR
Pattern

An answer choice contains a true-sounding fact that the passage never actually supports. Students who know the topic in real life often select these because the choice "sounds right" — but the passage does not say it.

Example

RC passage about Britain's Industrial Revolution. Choice: "Britain's colonial trade networks were the primary cause of industrialization." This is plausible in real history but the passage never argues it. Outside scope. Eliminate.

Defense

For RC and CR, the right answer is what the passage says or directly implies — not what is true in the world. If you find yourself reasoning "this is true in real life", that is a red flag. Return to the text.

Cross-reference: Ch. 05 · RC Question Types.

Trap · 07 The Opinion-as-Fact Trap

Multi-Source Reasoning · Reading Comprehension
Pattern

A tab or paragraph quotes a person stating an opinion, expectation, or projection — not a fact. The student treats the quoted statement as established truth.

Example

MSR Tab 1 (email): "We expect Q4 sales to grow 20%." Tab 2 (data): "Q4 actual growth: 8%." A question asks "what was Q4 growth?" The right answer is 8% (from data), not 20% (from email expectation).

Defense

Tag every statement in a tab as either FACT or OPINION on first read. For factual questions, use only the FACT tags. Opinion language: "we expect", "I believe", "it should", "we anticipate", "the team thinks".

Cross-reference: Ch. 07 · Conflicting Evidence.

Trap · 08 The Units Trap

Table Analysis · Word Problems · Graphics
Pattern

One column or quantity is in a different unit from another, but the units are not visually obvious. Mixed units (thousands vs. raw, percentage vs. count, hours vs. minutes) silently corrupt every calculation.

Example

A revenue column labeled "Revenue ($M)" — millions — and a customer column labeled "Customers" — raw counts. The student computes "revenue per customer" without converting and gets a value 1,000,000× too small.

Defense

Read every column header word for word, including parentheticals. For word problems, write units next to each variable when you define them. Always sanity-check your answer's order of magnitude — if a "revenue per customer" is $0.0003, you missed a unit.

Cross-reference: Ch. 08 · Table Analysis Traps.

Trap · 09 The Truncated-Axis Trap

Graphics Interpretation
Pattern

A bar chart's y-axis does not start at zero — it starts at some elevated value (e.g., 80). The visual difference between bars is exaggerated relative to the actual numerical difference. The student "eyeballs" the difference and mis-reads.

Example

Bars of height 90 and 100 plotted on an axis from 80 to 110 look like one bar is twice as tall as the other. The actual numerical difference is 10, or about 11% of the smaller bar — not 100%.

Defense

Read the y-axis numbers, not the bar heights. Compute every percentage difference numerically: (big − small) / small. Never eyeball the percent difference between bars on a truncated axis.

Cross-reference: Ch. 09 · Graphics Traps.

Trap · 10 The C-Trap

Data Sufficiency
Pattern

Two DS statements look incomplete individually and "obviously" need to be combined. Student picks C without testing each statement alone — and misses that one statement was sufficient by itself (so the answer is A, B, or D).

Example

Stem: "Is x > 0?" Statement 1: "x²> 0" (insufficient). Statement 2: "x³> 0" (sufficient — cubes preserve sign). Looks like you need both, but Statement 2 alone is sufficient. Answer: B, not C.

Defense

Always test each statement in isolation first. Combine only when both individually fail. The procedure is in Ch. 06 · DS Procedure. Run it every single time.

Trap · 11 The Sign-Flip Trap

Algebraic Logic · Inequalities
Pattern

The student multiplies or divides both sides of an inequality by a negative quantity but forgets to flip the inequality sign. Or worse, multiplies by a variable whose sign is unknown.

Example

Solve −2x < 6. Wrong: divide by −2 to get x < −3. Right: divide by −2 AND flip the sign to get x > −3.

Defense

Whenever you multiply or divide an inequality, ask "is the multiplier negative?" If yes, flip. If the multiplier is a variable of unknown sign, do not multiply at all — use a sign chart instead.

Cross-reference: Ch. 02 · Inequalities.

Trap · 12 The Less-Than Reversal Trap

Word Problems · Translation
Pattern

"5 less than x" gets translated as 5 − x instead of x − 5. The English phrase reverses the order; the student reads left-to-right and gets it backwards.

Example

"Anna is 5 years younger than Ben. Ben is 20." Wrong translation: Anna = 5 − 20 = −15. Right: Anna = 20 − 5 = 15.

Defense

Whenever you see "less than", "fewer than", "younger than", "shorter than", read the phrase twice and identify the subject versus the reference. The subject is what you are computing; subtract from the reference. "Anna is 5 less than Ben" → Anna = Ben − 5, not 5 − Ben.

Cross-reference: Ch. 03 · Translation Alphabet.
