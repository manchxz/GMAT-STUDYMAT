The Art of the Skip

Every minute spent on the wrong question is a minute stolen from the right one. The students who finish strong are not the smartest — they are the ones who know when to bail.

Why skipping is a skill, not a failure

Most students grade themselves on accuracy. The GMAT does not. The GMAT grades you on how many questions you answer correctly within a fixed time window. A question you spent four minutes on, missed, and then ran out of time on three other questions because of, costs you four wrong answers — not one. The skip is a strategic move designed to prevent that compounding loss.

This chapter is the chapter that lifts a 645 to a 705. The content of the previous nine chapters gives you the ceiling. This chapter is the discipline that lets you actually reach it on test day.

The strategic-skip core principles

  • Every section has a per-question time budget. Know yours cold.
  • No question on the GMAT is worth more than 2:30. Period.
  • A guessed question costs you nothing extra. A run-out-of-time question costs you 3–5 unattempted ones.
  • Bookmark and return — but only if you have time.
  • Practice the skip in your prep, not on test day. Build the muscle.

The 90-second rule

By the time you have spent 90 seconds on a Quant or Data Insights question with no clear path to the answer, the expected value of continuing collapses below the expected value of skipping. The math is brutal but simple.

Expert · the math behind the 90 seconds

If you are stuck at 90 seconds, your probability of solving correctly is roughly the same as guessing. So another 60 seconds spent thinking adds maybe 5–10 percentage points to your accuracy on this one question — but takes 60 seconds away from a future question where, if attempted with full time, you would have a much higher probability of getting it right. The trade is a near-certain loss.

Standard rule: when the timer hits 90 seconds and you do not have a clear path, eliminate as many choices as you can on instinct, guess from the remaining, and move.

ELI5 · two simple examples

Example 1 · the runner who slows down. A runner in a 10-lap race who spends 5 laps' worth of energy fighting through one tough lap is going to lose the next 4 laps badly. Better to give up some speed on the tough lap and save energy for the next ones. The GMAT clock is exactly that race.

Example 2 · the puzzle on the math worksheet. If you are stuck on Question 4 and there are 20 left, fighting Question 4 for ten minutes means you may not even read Questions 18, 19, 20. The teacher is going to grade based on total points, not on how hard you tried on number 4.

Sunk-cost detection — the hardest skill in the chapter

The hardest part of the skip is psychological. After spending 60 seconds on a problem, your brain says "I've already invested time, surely I can finish in just one more minute." That is the sunk-cost fallacy and it is the single biggest reason students miss their target score.

Expert · the sunk-cost prompt

When you find yourself thinking "just one more minute", ask yourself one question: "If I had not yet started this problem, would I now begin it with only the remaining time?"

If the answer is no — and at the 90-second mark on a stuck problem, the answer is almost always no — then continuing is the wrong move. Skip.

ELI5 · two simple examples

Example 1 · the half-eaten dinner. You order a meal at a restaurant, take three bites, and realize it tastes terrible. The sunk-cost fallacy says "I paid for it, so I should finish." The smart move is to stop eating and order something else. The money is gone either way; the only question is whether you also waste your stomach space.

Example 2 · the long line at the airport. You have been in a line at the airport for 20 minutes. You hear that the next gate over has no line. The sunk-cost voice says "but I've waited so long here." The right move is to walk to the empty line. You cannot get those 20 minutes back, but you can save the next 20.

The four skip categories

When to skip — and how

  • No-Idea skip → you read the question and have no entry point. Spend 15 seconds eliminating obvious wrong answers, then guess and move.
  • On-the-Fence skip → you have narrowed to two choices but cannot decide. Pick one, bookmark, move on. (Your gut at this point is roughly as good as another 60 seconds of thought.)
  • Careless-Risk skip → you can solve, but the calculation is fragile. If you have time, complete carefully. If not, decide between bailing and a guess.
  • Time-Pressure skip → you are behind schedule and must save time. Prefer skipping medium-length questions, not the shortest ones.

Section pacing — your per-question budgets

Focus Edition section budgets

  • Quant 21 questions, 45 minutes → ≈ 2:09 per question average. Target ≈ 2:00 to bank time.
  • Verbal 23 questions, 45 minutes → ≈ 1:57 per question average. RC passage sets eat more; CR is faster.
  • Data Insights 20 questions, 45 minutes → ≈ 2:15 per question. MSR sets are slower; GI faster.
Expert · the time-checkpoint system

Mid-section checkpoints prevent end-of-section panic. Memorize these targets:

Quant   · After Q11 → 22 minutes used (you have 23 left for 10 questions)
Verbal  · After Q12 → 23 minutes used (you have 22 left for 11 questions)
DI      · After Q10 → 22 minutes used (you have 23 left for 10 questions)

If you are off-pace by more than 2 minutes at the checkpoint, trigger a deliberate skip on the next medium-difficulty question to recover.

The Focus Edition bookmark / review feature

The Focus Edition allows you to bookmark up to three questions per section for return at the end. Use this feature deliberately:

Expert · what to bookmark, what not to bookmark
  • Do bookmark on-the-fence questions where you narrowed to two choices and need a fresh look.
  • Do bookmark a question where you suspect you misread but cannot afford the time right now.
  • Do not bookmark a no-idea question. You are unlikely to suddenly know more 20 minutes later.
  • Do not bookmark all three slots in the first 10 questions. Save bookmark capacity for later when stakes are higher.

Decision drills · 10 scenarios

For each scenario, choose the best decision. There is one objectively correct answer per scenario, given only the information described.

You are 15 minutes into Quant. You have answered 6 questions and the timer shows 30 minutes left for 15 more. The next question looks unfamiliar. Best decision?

  1. Spend 3 minutes to fully understand it before deciding.
  2. Skip immediately and guess.
  3. Set a 90-second mental timer; if no clear path by then, eliminate wrong choices and guess.
  4. Take a deep breath and re-read the previous question.
  5. Bookmark and start the next question.
Solution →

On a Data Sufficiency question, you have spent 60 seconds and narrowed the answer to C or E but cannot decide. Best decision?

  1. Continue analyzing until you are sure.
  2. Pick whichever feels closer to your last computation, bookmark, move on.
  3. Always pick C in this situation.
  4. Always pick E in this situation.
  5. Skip without picking.
Solution →

You are 38 minutes into Verbal. You have answered 21 of 23 questions. The next question is a long Reading Comprehension passage you have not started. Best decision?

  1. Read the entire passage carefully.
  2. Skim aggressively, focus on the question stems first, then dip into specific paragraphs as needed.
  3. Skip the passage and guess on both questions.
  4. Bookmark and try later.
  5. Take a 30-second break.
Solution →

You have 4 minutes left and 4 questions remaining in DI. You begin a Multi-Source Reasoning question, which has 3 sub-questions. Best decision?

  1. Read all three tabs in detail.
  2. Read tab headers only, scan for key entities, answer all three sub-questions in 4 minutes total.
  3. Pick the easiest-looking sub-question, answer it carefully, guess on the other two.
  4. Guess on all three sub-questions immediately.
  5. Skip the entire MSR scenario.
Solution →

You have already used 2 of your 3 bookmarks within the first 10 questions of a section. The 11th question is a moderately tricky inequality. Best decision?

  1. Bookmark it anyway.
  2. Solve it now or guess; preserve your last bookmark slot for a higher-stakes later question.
  3. Skip without bookmarking.
  4. Spend extra time to ensure correctness.
  5. Bookmark and skip.
Solution →

You realize you misread a question's wording (you treated "less than" as "greater than"). You are 75 seconds in. Best decision?

  1. Restart the question with the correct interpretation.
  2. Treat the time as sunk and submit your wrong-interpretation answer.
  3. Estimate quickly whether the correct interpretation flips your answer; if yes, redo; if no, submit and move.
  4. Bookmark.
  5. Take a break.
Solution →

You finish a section 8 minutes early with all 3 bookmarks used. Best decision?

  1. Submit early and rest.
  2. Return to bookmarked questions; review in priority order (highest-confidence-pending first).
  3. Re-read every question.
  4. Submit and use the time as a buffer between sections.
  5. Pick a random question and re-check.
Solution →

A Quant question has five answer choices. You can immediately rule out two as obviously wrong and have 60 seconds to evaluate the remaining three before your 90-second skip threshold. Best decision?

  1. Try to compute the exact answer in the remaining 60 seconds.
  2. Test plug numbers from the three remaining choices into the original; eliminate further. If still tied at 90 sec, guess.
  3. Always pick the middle choice.
  4. Skip immediately.
  5. Bookmark immediately.
Solution →

It is your test day. You discover you are 5 minutes behind pace at the Quant midpoint (Q11). Best decision?

  1. Slow down further to ensure quality on the remaining questions.
  2. Trigger one immediate strategic skip on the next moderately-hard question to recover.
  3. Skip the next 5 questions.
  4. Skip the easiest-looking remaining question to save time.
  5. Continue at the same pace.
Solution →

You have answered every question in a section. You have 4 minutes and 1 unused bookmark. Best decision?

  1. Submit immediately.
  2. Re-read every question's stem.
  3. Return to your bookmarked question and re-evaluate it carefully — even with one unused, that is the highest-EV question to revisit.
  4. Re-do the first question.
  5. Pick the question you spent the least time on and re-do.

Explanations

Explanation · 01↑ back to Q01
Answer · C

Set a hard mental timer and apply the 90-second rule. Spending 3 minutes (option A) violates the per-question budget. Skipping immediately (B) sacrifices easy elimination wins. C is the disciplined middle path.

Explanation · 02↑ back to Q02
Answer · B

50/50 between two choices means another minute of analysis adds little expected value. Pick, bookmark, return if time permits. C and D are bad heuristics — there is no reliable bias toward C or E in the actual exam.

Explanation · 03↑ back to Q03
Answer · B

You have 7 minutes for a passage with two questions. A full careful read takes too long; skipping (C) leaves easy points unattempted. The structural skim — read for archetype + map, then dive into the passage to answer specific questions — is the right move.

Explanation · 04↑ back to Q04
Answer · B

4 minutes for 4 questions = 1:00 per question. MSR rewards a fast triangulation read; full tab readings take too long. The only viable path is the rapid-skim approach.

Explanation · 05↑ back to Q05
Answer · B

Bookmarks are scarce capacity reserved for the questions you can most plausibly recover. Burning them early on moderate questions wastes them. Solve or guess the moderate one and preserve the slot.

Explanation · 06↑ back to Q06
Answer · C

Decide by expected value. If the misread does not affect your answer (rare but possible for "must be true" questions), submit and move. If it does, the cost of redoing is offset by the certainty of getting it right.

Explanation · 07↑ back to Q07
Answer · B

Bookmarked questions are the highest-leverage use of leftover time. Reviewing in priority order — start with the on-the-fence ones where new eyes are most likely to flip the answer — captures the most points per second.

Explanation · 08↑ back to Q08
Answer · B

Plugging answer choices into the question (back-solving) is far faster than computing the answer forward. With three choices remaining, two rounds of back-solving will usually identify the correct one in 60 seconds.

Explanation · 09↑ back to Q09
Answer · B

Slowing down further (A) makes the deficit worse. Skipping the easiest question (D) is wasteful. Skipping multiple questions (C) is overkill. The disciplined recovery is one strategic skip on a medium-difficulty question.

Explanation · 10↑ back to Q10
Answer · C

The bookmarked question is, by definition, the one most likely to flip with re-evaluation. It is a higher-expected-value use of 4 minutes than re-reading questions you already answered confidently or scanning the section blindly.
